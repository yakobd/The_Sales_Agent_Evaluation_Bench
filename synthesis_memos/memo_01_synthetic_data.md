# Synthesis Memo — Best Practices and Lessons Learned on Synthetic Data
**Liu et al., COLM 2024 | Yakob Dereje | TenX Academy Week 11 | April 29, 2026**

---

## What the Paper Argues

Liu et al. argue that synthetic data — training and evaluation data generated by LLMs
rather than collected from humans — can match or exceed human-labeled data quality, but
only when three conditions are met: the generation pipeline is diverse, every generated
example passes a verification step before entering the dataset, and the model used to
judge quality is different from the model used to generate. Teams that skipped any of
these three conditions consistently produced worse results than teams that used less data
but applied all three controls.

---

## The Design Choice I Disagree With

The paper recommends using a frontier model (GPT-4 class) as the primary generator for
domain-specific synthetic data, on the grounds that frontier models produce the highest
baseline quality and require less post-hoc filtering.

I disagree with this recommendation for domain-specific outreach generation tasks.

---

## My Disagreement — With Evidence From Week 10

A frontier model like GPT-4 is trained on internet-scale data. It has never seen the
Tenacious style guide, the 23 banned phrases list, the 60-character subject line rule,
or the ICP segment definitions that govern which outreach angle is appropriate for which
prospect signal.

My Week 10 probe library documents exactly what happens when a generator lacks this
domain context. Probe P06 shows the email agent asserting "aggressive hiring" for a
company with fewer than 5 open roles — a banned phrase that any internet-trained model
would consider acceptable professional language. Probe P10 shows gap language inserted
into an email before the competitor gap brief had been generated — a grounding failure
that only appears domain-critical if you know the Tenacious research credibility standard.
Probe P17 shows "just circling back" appearing in follow-up emails — a phrase that is
completely normal in generic business writing but is explicitly banned in the Tenacious
style guide.

GPT-4 would reproduce all three of these failures and its internal quality judgment would
not flag any of them as problems because it has no access to the domain standard against
which they are violations.

The paper's recommendation to trust the frontier model's quality judgment is therefore
wrong for this domain. The correct approach is the reverse: use a cheap model for bulk
generation and verify every output against a domain-specific mechanical filter — in my
case, the scoring_evaluator.py that runs 5 deterministic checks before any LLM judge
is consulted. The mechanical filter catches domain violations that no amount of frontier
model quality would prevent.

This is not a general disagreement with the paper — for generic domains where no
style guide exists, the frontier model recommendation is correct. But for any domain
with explicit, non-negotiable output constraints, domain-specific verification must
precede and override model quality judgment.

---

## How This Changes My Act II Approach

Instead of routing bulk generation through a frontier model, I will use Qwen3-Next-80B
(cheap dev-tier via OpenRouter) for generation and run every output through
scoring_evaluator.py before it enters the dataset. The frontier model (Claude Sonnet 4.6)
is reserved for the sealed held-out evaluation only — consistent with the paper's
contamination prevention guidance and the Week 11 cost discipline rules.

This means my generation cost stays under $3 for 200-300 tasks while my verification
quality is higher than the paper's recommended approach because it is domain-grounded.

---

## One Thing I Agree With Strongly — Diversity Is the Hardest Problem

Section 6 of the paper identifies diversity as the primary failure mode in synthetic
data pipelines. LLMs naturally repeat the same patterns — the same sentence structures,
the same argument shapes, the same difficulty levels — unless actively forced to vary.

My Week 10 trace pool confirms this. Of my 13 completed prospect threads, the majority
are Segment 1 (recently funded startups): Stripe, Notion, Rippling, Deel, Intercom,
Lattice, Brex, Linear, Retool, HubSpot. Only Yellow.ai, Deel, and Salesforce represent
Segment 2. Segment 3 (leadership change) has one company (Figma). Segment 4 (AI gap)
has one company (Retool). If I convert these traces directly into tasks without
rebalancing, approximately 70% of my dataset will be Segment 1 tasks and my trained
adapter will learn to write Segment 1 emails well while failing on the three other
segments.

The paper's diversity lesson directly informs my Act II partitioning strategy: I will
enforce a maximum of 25% per segment in the training partition, using programmatic
parameter sweeps to generate the underrepresented Segment 2, 3, and 4 examples that
my trace pool cannot provide in sufficient quantity.

---

## Summary

The paper's core contribution — filter aggressively, diversify actively, never use the
same model to generate and judge — is directly applicable to Tenacious-Bench v0.1.
My one disagreement is narrow and evidence-based: the frontier-model-as-generator
recommendation assumes generic domains. For domains with explicit mechanical constraints
like the Tenacious style guide, a cheap generator plus a domain-specific verifier
produces better-filtered data at lower cost than a frontier generator whose quality
judgment has no access to domain rules.
